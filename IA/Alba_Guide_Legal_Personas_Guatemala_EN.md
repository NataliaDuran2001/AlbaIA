**Formalizing a Business in Guatemala**

A simple, step-by-step guide to the legal personas — Alba · July 2026

*DISCLAIMER: This guide is for information only. It is not legal or tax advice. Costs and requirements change: always verify with the Registro Mercantil (registromercantil.gob.gt), SAT (sat.gob.gt) and minegocio.gt. Amounts in quetzales (Q), approximate as of July 2026\.*

# **Before you start: what you need for every option**

* A valid **DPI** (national ID).

* A **NIT** (tax ID) and a **SAT Agencia Virtual** account (free, online at portal.sat.gob.gt).

* An **email address** you actually check — your documents arrive there.

* A **utility bill** (water, electricity, phone) from the last 3 months proving the business address.

* Recommended: a **bank account**.

### **Which persona fits me?**

* I work alone, small business → **Comerciante Individual** (sole trader).

* I want a real company, fast and with no notary → **Sociedad de Emprendimiento**.

* We are 2–20 trusted partners (e.g. family) → **S.R.L. (limited liability company)**.

* I want investors or to grow big → **Sociedad Anónima (S.A.)**.

# **1\. Comerciante Individual — the simplest option**

**What it is:** you ARE the business. No separate company is created. Cheapest and fastest.

**Best for:** shops, sales, services, trades, single owner.

### **Steps**

1. Get your NIT for free at SAT’s Agencia Virtual.

2. Register in a tax regime (to start, **Pequeño Contribuyente** is usually best — see the tax section).

3. Activate **electronic invoicing (FEL)** in the Agencia Virtual. Mandatory and free.

4. If your working capital exceeds Q2,000: register as a merchant and register your **empresa mercantil** at the Registro Mercantil (online at minegocio.gt).

5. Pay the fees (approx. Q200) and receive your electronic **patente de comercio** (business license, approx. Q100).

6. If you have premises open to the public, signage, or sell food: get the permit from your **municipality**.

**Paperwork:** DPI, utility bill, online forms. **Total cost:** Q0 (SAT only) to \~Q300 (with Registro Mercantil). **Time:** 1–2 weeks.

### **Keep in mind**

* **You answer with your personal assets** (house, car) for business debts. This is the main downside.

* Easiest persona to close or change later.

* Even below the Registro Mercantil threshold, if you sell you must be registered with SAT and issue invoices.

# **2\. Sociedad de Emprendimiento (S.E.) — a company, 100% online, no notary**

**What it is:** a real company (separate from you), created online under Decree 20-2018. No public deed, no notary. **One shareholder is enough.**

**Best for:** entrepreneurs and startups that want liability protection without spending much.

### **Steps**

1. Request the **name certification** (denominación) at the Registro Mercantil.

2. Create an account at **minegocio.gt**.

3. Fill in the Sociedad de Emprendimiento application. The system generates the contract automatically.

4. Attach scans (JPG, PDF or PNG): each shareholder’s DPI (both sides) and a utility bill no older than 3 months.

5. Pay the fees online and wait for your registration and patente.

6. Register the company with SAT (it gets its own NIT), activate FEL, choose a tax regime.

**Cost:** low — a few hundred quetzales, no notary fees. **Time:** days to 2 weeks.

### **Keep in mind**

* Your liability is limited to what you contributed.

* It has an **annual revenue cap** (approx. Q5 million). If you outgrow it, you must transform into another company type. Verify the current amount when registering.

* Everything runs on electronic tools: Agencia Virtual, FEL, Declaraguate.

# **3\. Sociedad de Responsabilidad Limitada (S.R.L. / Ltda.)**

**What it is:** a company of **2 to 20 partners**, no shares. Business debts are paid only with company assets.

**Best for:** family businesses or a few trusted partners.

### **Steps**

1. Choose the name (must include "y Compañía Limitada" or "Ltda.").

2. Hire a **notary** to draft the public deed of incorporation. All capital must be **fully paid at signing**.

3. The notary files the deed with the Registro Mercantil (can be done online).

4. Pay fees and stamp taxes; receive the registration and patentes.

5. Register the company with SAT (own NIT), activate FEL, choose a regime.

6. Authorize the accounting and corporate books.

7. Municipal license if applicable.

**Cost:** notary fees (typically Q3,000–Q10,000+, varies) \+ registry fees and stamps. **Time:** 2–3 weeks.

### **Keep in mind**

* Selling or transferring your stake requires the other partners’ consent and a new deed.

* Full accounting required (you will need an accountant).

# **4\. Sociedad Anónima (S.A.) — for growth and investment**

**What it is:** the persona most used by mid-size and large companies. Capital is divided into **shares** that can be sold. Minimum paid-in capital: **Q200**.

**Best for:** businesses seeking investors or multiple shareholders.

### **Steps**

1. Choose the name and check availability at the Registro Mercantil.

2. Hire a **notary** to draft the deed of incorporation (capital, shares, administrator).

3. The notary files it with the Registro Mercantil and pays fees: Q275 base \+ Q6 per Q1,000 of authorized capital (max Q25,000) \+ Q15 edict \+ Q75 legal representative \+ Q100 company registration.

4. Pay fiscal stamps: Q250 (company patente) \+ Q50 (business patente).

5. You receive **2 patentes**: one for the sociedad, one for the empresa.

6. Register the S.A. with SAT (own NIT), FEL, tax regime.

7. Authorize corporate and accounting books; municipal license if applicable.

**Cost:** notary \+ roughly Q800+ in fees and stamps (rises with capital). **Time:** 10–20 business days.

### **Keep in mind**

* Yearly obligations: shareholders’ assembly, full accounting and, under the profit regime, the quarterly ISO tax.

* More expensive to maintain. Often too much to start small — you can begin as an S.E. and transform later.

Sociedad Colectiva and the Comandita types also exist but are rarely used today.

# **5\. Taxes: the 3 regimes you must know**

They apply to every persona. You pick one when registering with SAT and can change later.

### **A. Pequeño Contribuyente (Small Taxpayer) — the usual starting point**

* You pay **5% of gross sales** monthly. That 5% replaces both VAT and income tax.

* 2026 cap: **Q500,285 in sales per year** (Decree 31-2024; the cap rises yearly with the minimum wage).

* Only a **purchases-and-sales book** is required; invoice through FEL; file monthly via Declaraguate.

### **B. Simplified Optional Regime (on revenue)**

* Income tax: **5%** on the first Q30,000 per month, **7%** above that. You also charge **12% VAT**.

* For businesses over the small-taxpayer cap with few deductible expenses.

### **C. Profit Regime (Sobre las Utilidades)**

* Income tax: **25% on profit** (income minus documented expenses) \+ 12% VAT \+ quarterly ISO tax.

* Best with low margins and good expense records. Requires an accountant.

*Electronic invoicing (FEL) is mandatory for everyone. Selling without being registered, or without invoicing, brings fines.*

# **6\. Obligations people forget**

* **Municipal license** for premises and signage (each municipality has its own rules and rates).

* **Health license** from the Ministry of Health if you sell food.

* **IGSS (social security):** mandatory employer registration with 3+ workers (\~12.67% employer contribution on wages; 4.83% withheld from the worker).

* Written employment contracts and minimum wage.

* Keep your RTU data updated with SAT.

# **7\. The real barriers (beyond the law) and how to beat them**

Guatemalan studies (GEM, CIEN) show most people stay informal because they **see no benefit (\~40%)**, **think it costs too much (\~20%)** or **don’t know how (\~17%)**. These are the pains that come up most in practice:

* **"What’s in it for me?"** — Concrete benefits: invoicing large companies and the State, access to bank credit, no fines or closures, room to grow without fear.

* **Fear of SAT ("once I’m in, I can never leave")** — You can close: SAT has a definitive business cancellation process. Being up to date is what prevents problems, not staying outside.

* **Papers that don’t match** — The utility bill is in grandpa’s name, or the address differs from the DPI. Common fixes: a simple lease contract, a municipal certificate, or updating the RTU first.

* **"I need an accountant and can’t afford one"** — Under Pequeño Contribuyente you can do almost everything yourself: FEL is free on the SAT portal and the monthly filing is simple.

* **Queues, travel, and requirements that change by office** — Use the online routes (Agencia Virtual, minegocio.gt) whenever possible and keep screenshots of everything.

* **Limited internet or digital habits** — SAT agencies and kiosks help with filings; so do Mineco’s micro-enterprise promotion centers.

* **Middlemen ("tramitadores") who overcharge** — Official prices are public (Registro Mercantil fee schedule). If someone asks for much more, be suspicious.

* **Time** — Office hours are your business hours. Online procedures exist precisely for this.
